The read

Analysis — is the turnaround working?

The question

From 1 April 2026 Flat Stacks AU deliberately reset — a website redesign and a change of advertising strategy — pulling spend back hard while the new approach bedded in. The aim was not a wind-down: it was to fix the unit economics (conversion rate and MER), then re-scale spend profitably and grow revenue again, targeting an MER around 4.0 at materially higher spend. The read below is on one thing: is the data showing that working?

The headline MER gain is largely mechanical, not earned. Quarter-on-quarter, blended MER is essentially flat (2.62 Jan–Mar → 2.64 Apr–Jun); the rise people point to is the month-on-month climb off the April trough (2.32 → 2.66 → 2.98). Underneath it: returning-customer share jumped from 23.5% to 42.5%, new customers fell 78%, cost per new customer rose ~14% (A$86 → A$97), and platform ROAS actually fell (Meta 2.69 → 2.11; Google 8.01 → 6.28). Revenue dropped almost exactly in line with spend (both ~−75%), which tells us the paid spend was doing real, roughly-proportional work — the current efficiency is mostly a smaller business, not a discovered one.

What is actually carrying revenue — and lifting conversion — is the returning-customer base, not paid or organic. Paid Social (Meta) was the single biggest channel in March (A$42,288) and collapsed to A$3,269; Paid Shopping went to zero; Organic Search is now negligible (A$419 in June). Meanwhile returning customers held up: returning revenue went from 32.9% of tracked revenue (Mar) to 46.3% (Apr–Jun), and returning visitors convert at 3.5% against just 1.6% for new. That mix shift toward high-converting repeat buyers — reached largely through email (now ~22.8% of revenue and the highest-converting channel), brand search and direct — is what pulls the blended conversion rate back up. Note email itself isn't growing: its revenue is down in absolute terms (A$14,768 Mar → A$4,857 Jun); it just shrank slower than paid.

The lever a turnaround needs — new-visitor conversion — has not improved. New-customer CVR in June (1.6%) is still below March (1.7%) and July (1.9%). So the redesign has not yet made the site convert cold traffic better; the conversion-rate optics come from the audience mix, not the new experience. The redesign's clearest genuine win is data quality: a large pre-April "Untracked" revenue bucket (A$31,443, 27.2% of March, with impossible conversion rates) effectively disappeared from April — tracking was fixed. Good for measurement, but it also means pre/post GA4 figures aren't strictly comparable.

One encouraging signal. May is the single month that looks like the plan working: spend went up (A$8.4k → A$10.6k), new customers went up (77 → 111), AOV jumped (A$126 → A$163) and MER still improved — and it had the lowest returning-share of the quarter, so that gain wasn't a mix artifact. But June reverted to low-spend, returning-led mode, so it's one data point, not a trend.

The unbiased read. On this data the strategy is not yet proven to be working. The efficiency improvement is largely a smaller-spend, returning-customer mix effect; new-customer acquisition got more expensive and smaller; platform ROAS fell; and new-visitor conversion is still below pre-reset. The store is increasingly running on its existing base via email and brand — harvesting demand built earlier, not creating new demand. The genuine positives are narrow: cleaner tracking, a recovering returning-customer CVR, and the single May acquisition month. For the turnaround thesis to hold, two things still have to be demonstrated and neither is visible yet: (1) new-visitor conversion climbing above the pre-reset 1.7%–1.9%, and (2) MER holding near 4 as spend scales — today's number is flattered by spending only on the cheapest brand demand. The decisive test is a deliberate spend ramp (an extended May) that holds MER while new-customer CVR keeps climbing. Until that ramp happens and holds, continued investment is a bet on unproven leading indicators rather than demonstrated improvement.

Methodology

Data notes & definitions

  • Currency & period. All figures AUD. Monthly, July 2025 – June 2026; June 2026 is month-to-date (to the 25th), so June absolute totals are partial. Rates (MER, ROAS, conversion, AOV) are unaffected.
  • MER = Shopify net sales ÷ (Meta spend + Google spend), as specified. It excludes other channels/costs.
  • Platform vs Shopify revenue. Meta/Google "revenue" and ROAS use each platform's reported conversion value (platform-attributed, won't sum to Shopify). MER uses Shopify net sales as the source of truth.
  • Channel & customer-type figures are GA4 (purchaseRevenue; conversion rate = ecommercePurchases ÷ sessions). GA4 attribution differs from Shopify and from each ad platform. Pre-April "Untracked / (not set)" revenue is a GA4 tracking gap that the April redesign appears to have largely fixed, so pre/post GA4 comparisons aren't strictly like-for-like.
  • Google Ads shown as a total (the brand/non-brand split has been consolidated in the account).
  • Organic split. Brand vs non-brand traffic is Search Console clicks (query contains "flat stacks" = brand); ~45% of clicks are anonymised by Google ("Anonymised"). Organic revenue is GA4 Organic Search (not splittable by brand).
  • Sources. Shopify · Meta Ads acct 1787156028090681 · Google Ads acct 2052686950 · GA4 property 347982198 · Search Console flatstacks.com.au.
